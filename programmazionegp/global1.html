Teen Dietary Balance Project

Welcome to our team project! Our goal is to raise awareness among teenagers about healthy eating habits across different cultures.

Why a Balanced Diet Matters

A good diet is essential to maintain good health and well-being. In fact, a healthy diet ensures that your body gets the vitamins, minerals, proteins, fats, and carbohydrates it needs to function optimally. The foods we consume provide the building blocks for all bodily functions, affecting everything from energy levels to mental clarity.

Furthermore, if we consider children, teens, and pregnant women, proper nutrition is crucial for healthy growth, development, and reproduction. Nutrients like calcium, iron, and folic acid have a vital role in bone health and the development of the fetus.

A balanced diet helps maintain high energy levels throughout the day. It also helps reduce the risk of chronic conditions such as heart disease, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and certain types of cancer. It helps regulate cholesterol, blood sugar, and blood pressure, which are key factors in disease prevention.

General Tips:

  • Drink at least 1.5–2 liters of water per day.
  • Include protein in each meal to support muscle growth and overall development.
  • Avoid sugary foods, soda, and ultra-processed items.
  • Adjust portion sizes based on physical activity and energy needs.

Perspectives from Our Interviews

We recently interviewed a few friends to understand their approach to diet and physical activity. Their responses offer a glimpse into real-world eating habits and lifestyle choices among teenagers.

  • Giovanni: Maintains good physical condition despite a relaxed diet of biscuits, pasta, pizza, and occasional proteins. His active lifestyle and possibly fast metabolism may be helping him maintain balance, even without a strict diet plan.
  • Alice: Eats healthily and exercises consistently, yet is not seeing the results she hopes for. Possible factors include mismatched caloric intake, recovery issues, or a training plan not aligned with her specific fitness goals.
  • Marco: Relies on home-cooked meals without a fixed dietary plan but stays fit thanks to his daily training. His case suggests that consistent exercise can often compensate for a less-structured diet.

Current Trends in Italy

In Italy, healthy habits are not a strong priority among teens. According to a recent survey of 89,321 youths:

  • Only 7.5% exercise daily, while 13.8% never do any physical activity.
  • Screen time is rising: 26.5% of 17-year-olds spend over 5 hours a day on social media.
  • 26.8% skip breakfast entirely, while 12% eat sweets at least once per day.
  • Fruit and vegetable intake is alarmingly low: 6% never eat fruit, and 7.4% never eat vegetables.
  • Excess fats and sugars are consumed 2–4 times a week by 29.4% of teens, and daily by 26.2%.

A Global Snapshot

A WHO/Europe report reveals that healthy eating is declining among adolescents globally:

  • Less than 40% of teens eat fruits or vegetables daily, and the numbers drop with age.
  • 25% of teens consume sweets every day, with rates higher among girls.
  • 15% drink sugary soft drinks daily, especially among those from lower-income families.
  • Teens from less affluent backgrounds eat fewer fruits and vegetables and are more likely to be overweight or obese.

Dr. Martin Weber from WHO/Europe stresses that affordability and access are key barriers, often leading low-income families to rely on processed foods, which poses serious risks to adolescent health.
